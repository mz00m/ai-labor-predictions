📊 jobsdata.ai Weekly Update — Founding Week (Feb 23 - Mar 6, 2026)

Two weeks ago jobsdata.ai didn't exist. Today it tracks what the research actually says about AI's impact on jobs, wages, and workforce exposure across 17 prediction graphs backed by 316 verified sources. The founding sprint surfaced a pattern worth watching: the gap between projected displacement (6-8% of US jobs by 2030) and measured displacement (effectively 0% so far) remains wide, but the leading indicators are moving.

What was built:

🔬 Research & data
→ 316 sources ingested from scratch (172 peer-reviewed, 113 think tank/intl org, 23 major press, 8 blog/opinion) — 97% verified against original publications
→ Anthropic's labor market study found observed AI exposure across occupations but no aggregate unemployment rise, with young worker hiring showing the earliest slowdown
→ A batch ingest of 10 studies from Imas's AI productivity review brought in randomized trials from Google (-21% coding time), The Lancet (44% radiology workload reduction), and NBER (book output tripled post-ChatGPT, quality declined)
→ 142 plotted data points and 303 directional signals across all 17 prediction graphs

📈 Key data movements
→ AI adoption rate set at 17.5% based on Census Bureau BTOS biweekly survey data — a significant gap vs. McKinsey's 88% self-reported organizational adoption, explored in a new AdoptionLadder component that reconciles the two numbers
→ Tech-sector chart now splits observed vs projected trajectories: BLS projects programmer employment falling 9.6% through 2033 while software developer roles grow 17.9%, and Indeed posting data confirms a continued hiring freeze
→ A rigorous data quality audit removed fabricated entries, corrected sign errors, and deduplicated sources (319 → 296 references), improving the integrity of every weighted average on the site

🛠️ Site
→ Full dashboard with 17 prediction tiles, evidence tier filtering, weighted averages, and click-to-source chart interaction
→ /signals page tracks 64 AI tool packages across 10 industries with BLS employment overlay, organized into Now/Next/Later impact timelines
→ /history page contextualizes AI within four industrial revolutions, with compressed timelines reflecting 2-5x faster adoption than prior general-purpose technologies
→ Global source search across all 316 tracked studies
→ Automated weekly research digest pipeline pulling from OpenAlex, arXiv, Reddit, and job postings

By the numbers: 355 commits · 878 files changed · 316 sources · 142 data points · 303 directional signals · 17 prediction graphs · 6 pages · 37 components

The founding data raises a question that will define the next year of this initiative: if AI tool adoption is running at 2-5x the speed of prior general-purpose technologies, and entry-level hiring signals are already deteriorating (new grad tech hiring down 50% per Shih, entry-level postings down 23% per EIG), why hasn't aggregate employment moved? The answer likely sits in the Reduce/Amplify/Expand framework now on the signals page — most firms are amplifying output rather than reducing headcount. The question is how long that holds as AI capabilities compound.
